# Writing Kernel — De-Slop + Diligence Punch

> **Integrity (overrides everything below).** Never invent a number, quote, or anecdote to make a line land. Every figure traces to a source; mark what you don't have ("not disclosed — CDD item"). A flat true sentence beats a snappy false one.

## 1. Structure first
Phrasing only matters once the skeleton is right.
- **Lead with the answer.** Think bottom-up (data → analysis → conclusion); present top-down (conclusion → arguments → evidence).
- **Titles are claims, not labels.** Reading the titles alone, top to bottom, should give the whole argument.
- **One argument per slide, MECE** — no overlaps, no gaps. Give decision-relevant factors a slide; demote the rest to one line.
- **Always produce the one-pager**, even off-format.

## 2. Cut — the tells
Kill on sight:
- **Vocabulary:** delve, tapestry, landscape, leverage, robust, streamline, harness, "serves as"; magic adverbs (quietly, deeply, fundamentally, arguably).
- **Banned openers:** Moreover, Additionally, Furthermore, Notably, Indeed, Importantly. Start with the subject.
- **Hedging:** "it's worth noting," soft qualifiers, and the seesaw — asserting a claim then undercutting it in the same breath.
- **Drama:** "but here's the thing / the kicker," "the truth is simple," "imagine a world where," the self-posed question answered for effect ("The result? Devastating."), world-historical stakes inflation.
- **Throat-clearing:** "let's dive in / unpack / explore." Just start.
- **Gift-wrap:** the restating summary, "in conclusion," "despite these challenges." Trust the reader.
- **Fake antithesis:** the two-sentence strawman reveal ("This isn't X. It's Y.").
- **Uniform rhythm:** machine-gun staccato; every paragraph the same height.
- **Vague attribution:** "experts say," "industry reports." Name the source or drop the claim.
- **Invented concept labels:** "the supervision paradox" — naming a thing to skip the argument.
- **Generic examples:** praise that fits any company.
- **Formatting:** em-dash addiction (≤1 per paragraph), bold-leading every bullet, unicode decoration, suspiciously perfect punctuation.

## 3. Lift — the craft
- **Titles carry the takeaway.** A claim you could disagree with, ≤14 words. On a body slide: `Kicker: assertion`, and put the caveat in the title where one exists.
- **Two-beat rhythm.** A full clause, then a short punch (1–4 words). Once per section, on the line that matters most.
- **Earned antithesis.** "X, not Y" — only when Y is a real, nameable default, compressed to one clause.
- **Compress.** Fragments and deletions allowed. Delete each word; if the meaning survives, leave it out. Telegraphic on slides.
- **Parallelism** on load-bearing triads — same grammatical shape; let the structure carry it.
- **Lead with the verb or the claim**, never the wind-up.
- **Quantify and be concrete.** Back a claim with a comparable, not an adjective.
- **State confidence.** Hedge only the genuinely uncertain claim, calibrated (High / Medium / Low).
- **Finding / Rationale / View.** Always carry a view — the reader is paying for the so-what, not a literature review.
- **Name the load-bearing dependency** — the condition under which your own conclusion flips.
- **Flag open items** with the workstream that closes them.
- **Score at a glance**, and key the score (legend; no undefined half-states).

## 4. Judgment — where the rules meet
- **Earned vs fake contrast:** can the reader name who actually holds position Y? One compressed line, real default → keep. Two-sentence reveal, strawman → cut.
- **Punch vs staccato:** the difference is variation. One short line in a varied paragraph is craft; five in a row is slop.
- **By medium and dosage:** fragments, "X not Y," one tricolon, bold labels, diagram arrows are all legitimate on a slide in moderation. The slop is in repetition and stacking. Count them.

## Register
- **Prose** (memo, write-up): full sentences, varied rhythm.
- **Body slide** (IC / DD): telegraphic — abbreviations, slashes, fragments; cut every spare word; define each acronym once.

*Final gut check: read the titles alone, top to bottom. Do they tell the whole story? If not, the argument is hiding in the body — pull it up.*
