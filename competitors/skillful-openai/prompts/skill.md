# MBB Language

A house standard for making written output read partner-grade: structured, de-slopped, and sharp. The default target is the **investment-committee / due-diligence register** — the language that ships to a PE fund. Memo prose and marketing voice are secondary registers handled the same way with lighter compression.

The work runs in four steps, **in order**: get the skeleton right, strip the AI tells, sharpen what's left, and reconcile the rules where they collide. Order matters — no amount of de-slopping rescues a buried argument, and a sentence that is merely *clean* is only half done. The target is clean **and** lands.

## Integrity rule — overrides everything below

Never invent a statistic, fabricate a quote, or manufacture an anecdote to make a line land. In diligence, every number traces to the CIM, the data room, or a named source; every customer quote is real and attributable. A flat true sentence beats a snappy false one. When the figure isn't there, say so ("not disclosed — DD item") rather than inventing one. Snappiness is never a licence to fabricate.

## Choose the register first

Pick the register before touching the words — it sets how hard to compress.

| Deliverable | Register | Core rule |
|---|---|---|
| **IC / DD body slide, exec summary** (the spine) | **Telegraphic** | Compress hard: abbreviations, slashes, clipped noun-phrases, fragments. `Kicker: assertion` titles. Always carry an explicit point of view. |
| Memo, write-up, storyline narrative, leave-behind | Clean prose | Full sentences, varied rhythm, fully de-slopped. |
| Marketing / positioning | Declarative voice | Compressed, contrast-driven, quantified, unafraid of a fragment. |

The most common failure is writing slide content in the *prose* register — too long for a real body slide. When in doubt for a deck, default to telegraphic.

## Step 1 — Structure before style (Pyramid Principle)

Phrasing only matters once the skeleton is right.

- **Lead with the answer.** You *think* bottom-up (data → analysis → conclusion); you *present* top-down (conclusion → arguments → data). State the recommendation first, then support it. Directness reads as confidence.
- **Three levels:** the answer on top; a handful of supporting arguments beneath (three is the classic number); evidence under each.
- **Titles are claims, not labels.** Write titles so that reading them alone, top to bottom, gives the whole argument. "AI reshapes the target's pricing power" is a claim; "Market overview" is a label. If a title could sit above any slide in any deck, rewrite it.
- **Group MECE.** Supporting arguments are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive — no overlaps, no gaps. One body slide per argument; nothing orphaned.
- **Always produce the one-pager.** Even off-format, build a tear-off executive summary. Forcing the work onto one page proves you know the key points.
- **When *not* to use it:** an interim fact-share (you're walking a counterpart through information, not recommending), or a standalone leave-behind meant to stand on its own as longer prose.

## Step 2 — De-slop (strip the AI tells)

Remove the machine fingerprints before sharpening. The full catalogue with before/after pairs is in `references/de-slop-flags.md` (the 12 core flags) and `references/trope-catalogue.md` (the extended set). Read those when running a real pass. The families to watch:

- **Phrasing tells:** em-dash crutches, fake "not X but Y" antithesis, dramatic pivots ("but here's the thing"), soft hedging ("it's worth noting").
- **Rhythm tells:** uniform staccato, cookie-cutter equal-height paragraphs, gift-wrapped summaries, throat-clearing openers ("let's dive in").
- **Authenticity tells:** suspiciously perfect punctuation, copy-paste metaphors, overexplaining the obvious, generic any-company examples.

Kill-on-sight vocabulary (delve, tapestry, landscape, leverage-as-verb, robust, etc.) lives in `references/banned-words.md` — cheap to scan against any draft.

## Step 3 — Sharpen to MBB standard

De-slopping makes a draft clean; this makes it *land*. The full set of moves with worked examples is in `references/mbb-craft.md`. Read it when sharpening. The diligence-spine moves, in short:

- **`Kicker: assertion` titles** that carry the verdict — and the caveat where one exists, in one line.
- **Lead with the answer, then deep-dive only on the decision-relevant questions.** Don't give every factor a slide; give the load-bearing ones a slide and demote the rest to one line in the summary.
- **Finding / Rationale / View** — always carry an explicit View. The reader is paying for the so-what, not a literature review.
- **Telegraphic compression** — cut every spare word; define each acronym once; don't collapse into jargon-soup.
- **Concrete comparable, not adjective** — show a specific analogue set, don't assert "much faster."
- **Name the load-bearing dependency** — state the condition under which your own conclusion flips, before the room raises it.
- **Flag open items** with the workstream that closes them. Calibrated honesty reads as rigour, not weakness.
- **Scored verdicts** (traffic-light dots) with a legend and no undefined half-states.

General craft underneath the spine: action titles as claims; two-beat rhythm (long clause, short punch); earned "X, not Y" contrast; fragments and deletions allowed; parallel structure on triads; lead with the verb; quantify concretely; state confidence rather than padding it.

## Step 4 — Reconcile the conflicts

The de-slop layer and the MBB layer genuinely contradict in places (de-slop bans antithesis; MBB craft is built on it). Judgement resolves them. Full logic in `references/reconciliation.md`. The two tests that matter most:

- **Antithesis — can the reader name who holds position Y?** If Y is a real, specific default (a status quo, a named competitor approach) and you can compress it to one clause, the contrast is *earned* — keep it. If Y is a strawman nobody believed, or it sprawls across two sentences as a "this isn't… it's…" reveal, it's fake — cut it.
- **Punch vs staccato — is there variation?** One short punch in a varied paragraph is craft. Five short sentences in a row is slop. The difference is rhythm, not the fragment itself.

**Dosage principle (covers the whole trope catalogue):** any single trope once is usually fine; slop is in repetition and stacking. One earned "X, not Y", one two-beat punch, one tricolon per slide reads sharp. Ten of each reads like a machine. Count them.

## Pre-flight checklist

Run a near-final draft through this, in order.

**Cut (de-slop):**
- Em-dashes down to ≤1 per paragraph
- No "but here's the…", "let's dive/unpack", "it's worth noting"
- No two-sentence strawman antithesis (apply the Step-4 test)
- No gift-wrap summary; no overexplaining; examples specific not generic

**Lift (sharpen):**
- Every title a claim you could disagree with, ≤14 words
- One two-beat punch on the line that matters most
- Contrasts earned — Y nameable in 2–3 words
- Spare words deleted; parallel structure on key triads
- Leads with verb/claim; concrete and quantified where it counts
- Hedge only the genuinely uncertain claim

**If it's an IC / DD body slide:**
- Title is `Kicker: assertion` and carries the caveat where one exists
- Exec summary runs Finding / Rationale / View — and View states a view
- Telegraphic, not prose; acronyms defined once
- Claims backed by a concrete comparable
- Load-bearing dependency named; open items flagged with their workstream
- Any scoring has a legend; no undefined half-states

**Final gut check:** read the titles alone, top to bottom. Do they tell the whole story? If not, the argument is hiding in the body — pull it up.

# De-slop flags — the hygiene layer

The baseline. None of this should survive into a final draft. Twelve flags in three families. Each has a flagged version (🚩) and a fixed version (✅). Examples are illustrative, not real deliverable content.

> Several of these have an *earned* counterpart in MBB craft (antithesis, the short punch). Those are handled in `reconciliation.md`. The flags here are the manufactured, slop versions only.

## Phrasing flags

**1. Em dashes as a crutch.** The long dash jammed mid-sentence to bolt on an aside or fake emphasis. One is fine; three in a paragraph is a tic.
- 🚩 "The team — already stretched thin — had no capacity to mentor. The result — predictably — was chaos."
- ✅ "The team was already stretched thin, with no capacity to mentor. The result was predictable."

**2. Corrective antithesis (the fake kind).** "Not X. But Y." Sets up something the reader never assumed, then corrects it for drama. *(There is an earned version — see reconciliation.md. The flag here is only the manufactured, two-sentence strawman.)*
- 🚩 "This isn't because they don't trust the technology. It's because they can't predict it."
- ✅ "They trust the technology fine. What they can't do is predict it — and that's what keeps them up at night."

**3. Dramatic pivot phrases.** "But here's the thing." "Here's the catch." A written drumroll before something that didn't need one.
- 🚩 "The patterns are valuable. But here's the bind: capturing them costs more than most could justify."
- ✅ "The patterns are valuable, but capturing them costs more than most could justify. So they don't."

**4. Soft hedging.** "Something we've observed." "This is where X really shines." "It's worth noting that." Length without meaning — the sound of trying not to offend instead of trying to be clear.
- 🚩 "It's worth noting that this approach has shown some promising results in certain contexts."
- ✅ "This approach works. We've seen it across a dozen deployments."

## Rhythm flags

**5. Staccato on repeat.** Short sentence. Another short sentence. And another. Every sentence the same weight, so none of them land. *(Deliberate two-beat staccato is an MBB tool — see mbb-craft.md B2. The flag is uniform machine-gun fire, not purposeful punch.)*
- 🚩 "Agents act. They send emails. They modify code. They book appointments. They update databases."
- ✅ "Agents are starting to do real things now, not just answer questions — they'll send an email on your behalf or update a database before you notice."

**6. Cookie-cutter paragraphs.** Every paragraph the same height: three sentences, four, three, four. Human writing varies. Some ideas need a paragraph; some need one line.

**7. Gift-wrapped endings.** The reflexive summary that restates everything and adds "moving forward." Trust the reader to remember.
- 🚩 "In summary, by focusing on clear communication, consistent feedback, and mutual trust, teams can build stronger relationships and deliver better results."
- ✅ "The best teams never talked about trust. They just had it."

**8. Throat-clearing.** "Let's explore." "Let's unpack." "Let's dive in." "In this article we'll…" Just start.
- 🚩 "In this section, we'll explore the hidden costs of poor data hygiene. Let's dive in."
- ✅ "Poor data hygiene cost the last target two months of integration work."

## Authenticity flags

**9. Perfect punctuation.** Not a rule broken anywhere — no fragments, no comma splices, nothing starting with "And" or "But." Reads like a style guide proofread it. Good writing bends rules when bending them sounds better.

**10. Copy-paste metaphors.** A metaphor found and then repeated word-for-word until it dies (the same "moat" image, identically phrased, four times). Use it once; trust the reader to carry it.

**11. Overexplaining the obvious.** Spelling out what the reader already knows, like explaining how a door works before letting them through.
- 🚩 "Trust is earned over time. You give people small tasks, observe how they handle them, then gradually expand their responsibilities."
- ✅ "Trust is earned. The question is whether you're giving people the chance to earn it."

**12. Generic examples.** Surface praise that fits any company. No insider knowledge, no specificity. Reads like a landing page.
- 🚩 "Take the market leader. By focusing on seamless customer experience, they transformed the category."
- ✅ "The leader won by ignoring the feature race entirely and shipping the one integration every buyer's procurement team already required."

# MBB craft — the sharpening layer

De-slopping removes the tells; it does not make writing *good*. These are the moves that make a clean draft land. The **diligence / IC register comes first** because it's the spine — it's the language that ships to a fund. General craft follows; it applies to all registers.

All examples are illustrative and generic. Where a number appears, treat it as a placeholder — in a real deliverable it must trace to a source (see the Integrity rule in SKILL.md).

---

## Part 1 — The diligence / IC register (the spine)

This is denser and more clipped than prose. When the deliverable is an IC or DD slide, this is the register.

### D1. Title formula: `Kicker: assertion`
Each body slide opens with a short highlighted kicker, a colon, then a full-sentence claim that *is* the takeaway. The strongest titles state the verdict and flag what's still open, in one line — calibrated confidence done in the headline.
- "Executive summary: Limited AI disruption risk; upside across opex, TAM expansion and credentialisation."
- "Moat vs AI: Generative tools can draft the output today, but the regulatory sign-off is what buyers actually pay for."
- "Tech capability: No red flags in delivery to date; team depth and architecture to confirm in TDD."

The third carries both the answer and the caveat — that's the model to copy.

### D2. Lead with the answer; deep-dive only on what matters
An exec summary plus two deep-dives beats forty slides. Pick the load-bearing questions (is the moat real against AI? can the team execute?) and spend the pages there. Everything else is one line in the summary. Pyramid discipline in practice: give the *decision-relevant* factors a slide, demote the rest.

### D3. Three columns: Finding / Rationale / View
The exec summary runs the assertion (finding), the evidence (rationale), and an explicit point of view (the View). The View column is the differentiator — it's judgement, not synthesis. Always carry a View; a diligence reader is paying for the so-what.

### D4. Telegraphic register: compress harder than prose
Body slides cut far more than running text. Abbreviations, slashes and clipped noun-phrases are the default: "w/o major red flags," "prod/dev ratio," "c. 48% margin," "+64 NPS," ">114% NRR," "29 integrations across the main platform categories." On a body slide, if a word can go, it goes. One caveat: define each acronym once, and don't let density collapse into jargon-soup an IC reader can't parse.

### D5. Find a concrete comparable; don't assert
Don't argue "AI won't erode pricing." Lay out a specific analogue set — several adjacent regulated or assurance-led industries — mark where pricing held versus collapsed, and draw the line back to the target. A specific comparable set beats any adjective.

### D6. Name the load-bearing dependency out loud
Close the argument on the condition under which your own conclusion flips: "this only holds if the purchase rationale is assurance; if the buyer's real concern is output quality, pricing comes under pressure." Stating it signals rigour and pre-empts the sharpest pushback before the room raises it.

### D7. Flag open items; don't bury them
Tag rows "further diligence required (org chart, squad set-up, cadence)." Say what you know, mark what you don't, name the workstream that closes it. Calibrated honesty reads as confidence.

### D8. At-a-glance verdict scoring
Traffic-light dots give the skim-reader the verdict per row before they read a word. Always include a legend, and don't use ambiguous half-states (a half-green/half-amber dot) without defining them. Score, but key the score.

## Part 2 — General craft (all registers)

### B1. The action title carries the takeaway, not the topic
Every headline is a **claim you could agree or disagree with**, not a label. The reader should read titles alone and get the argument.
- "AI is reshaping how this category competes" — a position, not "Market context."
- "The defensible asset is the data, not the software" — an argument, not "Our view of the moat."

> **Formula:** `[Subject] + [active verb] + [specific object]`, optionally + an earned contrast. One idea. Aim ≤ 12–14 words. If the title could sit above any slide in any deck, it's a label — rewrite it into a claim.

### B2. Two-beat rhythm — long claim, then short punch
A full clause sets it up; a fragment lands it. The punch is usually 1–4 words.
- "It does your best work. Faster."
- "Every firm has them. Good for credibility, low on value."

This is *not* flag #5 (staccato-on-repeat); it's deliberate, used once, on the line that matters most. Once per slide or section.

### B3. Earned antithesis ("X, not Y") — the core positioning move
- "experience and judgement, not old decks"
- "built for the workflow, not retro-fitted from a chat tool"
- "what you delivered, not how"

It works because Y is a **real, specific default** the audience actually holds, compressed to one line. See reconciliation.md for the rule that separates this from the fake version.

### B4. Compression — fragments and deletions are allowed
Trust fragments; cut every spare word.
- "Repeatable, but rigid."
- "Static, out of date, low value."

Test: delete each word; if the meaning survives, leave it deleted. This is the opposite of hedging (#4) and overexplaining (#11) — same goal, pushed further.

### B5. Parallelism for the load-bearing lists
When three things sit together, give them the same grammatical shape and let the structure do the work.
- "Create / Improve / Standardise" — verb-led, parallel labels.
- "Agents run whole workflows / clients expect more, faster / incumbents aren't catching up" — three subject-verb claims, same cadence.

### B6. Lead with the verb or the claim, never the wind-up
No "We believe that…", no "It is the case that…". Open on the subject doing something. Front-load the load-bearing word.

### B7. Quantify, and make it concrete
Punch comes from specificity. Not "much faster" — "cut the per-stage assessment from days to under 30 minutes." Numbers, named entities and concrete nouns beat adjectives. (This is also the cure for generic examples, flag #12.)

### B8. Confidence — state it, don't pad it
Declarative, present tense, no "may" or "could" unless the uncertainty is the point. In diligence you *will* need calibrated hedges (confidence levels, "subject to"), but hedge **the claim that's genuinely uncertain**, not every sentence out of reflex.

# Reconciliation — the judgement layer

The de-slop layer and the MBB layer genuinely contradict. De-slop bans corrective antithesis (flag #2); MBB craft is built on antithesis (B3). De-slop bans staccato (flag #5); MBB craft prescribes the short punch (B2). Both sides are right — they describe different things. Judgement, not a rule, resolves them.

## Test 1 — Antithesis: can the reader name who holds position Y?

|  | **Earned contrast — USE it** | **Fake antithesis — CUT it** |
|---|---|---|
| Y is… | a real default, status quo, or named competitor approach | a strawman nobody actually believed |
| Length | one compressed line | two sentences: setup, then "correction" |
| Purpose | sharpen positioning | manufacture a reveal / fake drama |
| Example | "built for the workflow, not retro-fitted from a chat tool" | "This isn't because they don't trust it. It's because they can't predict it." |

**Decision rule:**
- ✅ Keep the contrast if Y is specific and real, and you can compress it to a single clause after a comma or semicolon.
- 🚩 Kill it if it spans two sentences, opens with "This isn't… / It's not that…", or sets up a belief no one held.

When in doubt: write the positive claim first. Add the "not Y" only if Y names something concrete the audience would otherwise assume. If you can't name Y in two or three specific words, you don't have an earned contrast — you have padding.

## Test 2 — Punch vs staccato: is there variation?

Flag #5 bans *uniform* short sentences. B2 *prescribes* an occasional short punch. The difference is variation. One punch line in a varied paragraph is craft. Five in a row is slop.

## C2 — Reconciling the trope catalogue with the slide medium

The extended trope catalogue (`trope-catalogue.md`) is a strict "write like a human" standard built for blog prose. Most of it is universal — kill "delve," "tapestry," "serves as," vague attributions, grandiose stakes, "despite its challenges," signposted conclusions, em-dash addiction, on sight, everywhere. But a few tropes it bans are **legitimate slide-craft moves in moderation**. Resolve the conflict by medium and by dosage.

| Trope | In running prose (memo, write-up) | On a slide / in a title |
|---|---|---|
| **Short punchy fragments** | Avoid as standalone paragraphs; vary sentence length | **Allowed and encouraged** — fragments are native to slides ("Repeatable, but rigid."). Cap at the one or two that carry weight |
| **Negative parallelism ("X not Y")** | Use rarely; apply Test 1; never the two-sentence reveal | **Allowed when earned** (B3) — a core title move. One per title; Y must be nameable |
| **Tricolon (rule of three)** | One per section, never stacked | One parallel triad per slide is fine (B5); don't stack three triads on one slide |
| **Bold-first bullets** | Avoid in prose paragraphs | Fine on slides where the bold word is a genuine label; vary it — don't bold-lead every line, and never bold a whole sentence |
| **Unicode arrows / smart quotes** | Use straight quotes; spell out "to" | Arrows acceptable in slide diagrams (Input → Output); avoid in body text |

## The dosage principle (the whole catalogue in one line)

*Any single trope once is fine; the slop is in repetition and stacking.* A deck with one earned "X, not Y" title, one two-beat punch and one tricolon reads sharp. The same deck with ten of each reads like a machine. Count them.

# Extended trope catalogue

A fuller reference layer, drawn from [tropes.fyi](https://tropes.fyi) and the [anti-ai-slop-writing](https://github.com/jalaalrd/anti-ai-slop-writing) skill (which itself draws on Carnegie Mellon 2025, Wikipedia's "Signs of AI writing," and large-scale post analyses). Read alongside `reconciliation.md` C2, which governs the handful that conflict with slide craft.

**Rule of thumb:** any one of these once may be fine; the tell is multiple tropes together, or one used repeatedly.

## Word choice

**Magic adverbs.** Adverbs that fake significance: *quietly, deeply, fundamentally, remarkably, arguably.*
- 🚩 "quietly orchestrating workflows" · "a quiet intelligence behind it"

**"Delve" and friends.** *delve, certainly, utilize, leverage (verb), robust, streamline, harness.*
- 🚩 "Let's delve into the details" · "We need to leverage these robust frameworks."

**Grandiose nouns for simple ideas.** *tapestry, landscape, paradigm, synergy, ecosystem, framework.*
- 🚩 "the rich tapestry of experience" · "navigating the complex landscape of modern AI."

**The "serves as" dodge.** Pompous swaps for "is/are": *serves as, stands as, marks, represents.*
- 🚩 "The platform serves as a reminder of…" → "The platform is…"

## Sentence structure

**Negative parallelism.** "It's not X — it's Y." The single most-identified AI tell. Includes "not because X, but because Y," the em-dash dismissal "X — not Y," and the cross-sentence reframe "The question isn't X. The question is Y." *(An earned, compressed version is a valid slide move — see reconciliation.md.)*
- 🚩 "It's not bold. It's backwards."

**"Not X. Not Y. Just Z."** The dramatic countdown faking a narrowing toward truth.
- 🚩 "Not a bug. Not a feature. A fundamental design flaw."

**"The X? A Y."** Self-posed rhetorical question, answered immediately for drama.
- 🚩 "The result? Devastating." · "The worst part? Nobody saw it coming."

**Anaphora abuse.** Same sentence opening repeated in quick succession.
- 🚩 "They assume users will pay… They assume developers will build… They assume…"

**Tricolon abuse.** Rule-of-three overused or stacked. One is elegant; three back-to-back is a pattern failure. *(One per slide is fine — see reconciliation.md.)*

**"It's worth noting."** Filler transitions that connect nothing: *it bears mentioning, importantly, interestingly, notably.*

**Superficial -ing analyses.** A trailing "-ing" phrase bolting on hollow significance: *highlighting its importance, reflecting broader trends, underscoring its role.*

**False ranges.** "From X to Y" where X and Y aren't on any real scale.
- 🚩 "From innovation to implementation to cultural transformation."

## Paragraph structure

**Short fragments for manufactured emphasis** (in prose). *(Native to slides — see reconciliation.md.)*
- 🚩 "He published this. Openly. In a book."

**Listicle in a trench coat.** A list disguised as prose with "The first… The second… The third…"

## Tone

**False-suspense transitions.** *here's the thing, here's where it gets interesting, here's what most people miss, here's the kicker.*

**"Think of it as…"** The patronizing analogy. Teacher mode; often less clear than the original.

**"Imagine a world where…"** The futurism invitation followed by a wish-list.

**False vulnerability.** Polished, risk-free "honesty" that performs authenticity.
- 🚩 "This is not a rant; it's a diagnosis."

**"The truth is simple."** Asserting clarity instead of proving it.
- 🚩 "The reality is simpler and less flattering."

**Grandiose stakes inflation.** Everything is world-historical.
- 🚩 "This will fundamentally reshape how we think about everything."

**Pedagogical hand-holding.** *let's break this down, let's unpack, let's explore, step by step.*

**Vague attributions.** Unnamed authorities and inflated counts: *experts, observers, industry reports, several publications.* If you can't name the source, you don't have one.

**Invented concept labels.** Abstract problem-nouns appended to domain words and used as if established: *the supervision paradox, the acceleration trap.* Names a thing to skip the argument.

## Formatting

**Em-dash addiction.** 20+ where a human uses 2–3.

**Bold-first bullets.** Every list item opening with a bolded phrase. *(Tolerable on slides as labels; avoid in prose; never bold a whole sentence.)*

**Unicode decoration.** Curly quotes and arrows where straight characters belong. *(Arrows OK in slide diagrams only.)*

## Composition

**Fractal summaries.** "Tell them what you'll tell them / tell them / tell them what you told them" at every level.

**The dead metaphor.** One metaphor beaten flat across the whole piece.

**Historical analogy stacking.** Rapid-fire historical companies/revolutions for borrowed authority.
- 🚩 "Apple didn't build the ride app. The social network didn't build the music app."

**One-point dilution.** One thesis restated ten ways across thousands of words to feel "comprehensive."

**Content duplication.** Whole paragraphs repeated verbatim — an unedited-output tell.

**The signposted conclusion.** "In conclusion / to sum up / in summary."

**"Despite its challenges…"** The fixed beat: "Despite its [positives], [subject] faces challenges… Despite these challenges, [optimism]."

## Additional flags

**Banned sentence openers.** *Certainly, Moreover, Additionally, Furthermore, Notably, Indeed, Importantly.* Start the sentence with its subject instead.

**Hedging seesaw.** Asserting a claim, then undercutting it in the same breath: "This is a major risk, though it may not materialise and could equally be an opportunity." Pick a position. Hedge once, deliberately, with a stated confidence level (High / Medium / Low) — which is exactly what the diligence register already does.

**Corporate pep talk and passive voice.** "Mistakes were made." Name the actor; use the active voice.

**Invented statistics, quotes, anecdotes.** The Integrity rule, repeated as an accuracy failure: never fabricate to strengthen a point. Non-negotiable in diligence.

*Write like a human: varied, imperfect, specific.*

# Banned words — kill-on-sight vocabulary

> **Generated list — prune before relying on it.** Compiled from the words named across the de-slop and trope sources (tropes.fyi, anti-ai-slop-writing) plus the words that recur in AI-detection studies. It is deliberately broad. Some entries are context-dependent (a banned word can be the right word in a quote, a proper noun, or a defined technical term), so treat this as a flag list, not an automatic delete. Scan a draft against it; for each hit, ask whether the word is doing real work or padding.

**How to use:** a single hit is a prompt to look, not a failure. Density is the signal — several of these in one paragraph almost always means the draft is slopped. Strip fenced code, inline code, URLs and defined acronyms before scanning so technical content doesn't false-positive.

## Tier 1 — near-always slop (strongest signal)

delve, tapestry, landscape (as metaphor), testament (to), vibrant, pivotal, realm, intricate, multifaceted, nuanced (as filler), robust (as filler), seamless, seamlessly, leverage (verb), utilize, harness, streamline, synergy, ecosystem (as metaphor), paradigm, holistic, underscore, underscores, foster (as filler), navigate (as metaphor), unlock (as metaphor), elevate, empower, supercharge, revolutionize, transformative, game-changer, cutting-edge, state-of-the-art, best-in-class, world-class.

## Tier 2 — filler adverbs and intensifiers

quietly, deeply, fundamentally, remarkably, arguably, notably, importantly, interestingly, crucially, significantly (as filler), truly, genuinely, simply, essentially, ultimately, effectively (as filler), incredibly, undoubtedly, certainly (as opener).

## Tier 3 — banned sentence openers

Certainly, Moreover, Additionally, Furthermore, Notably, Indeed, Importantly, Ultimately, Essentially, In essence, In today's [X] world, In the realm of, When it comes to, It's worth noting that, It's important to note that, Needless to say.

## Tier 4 — pompous verb swaps for "is / are / shows"

serves as, stands as, marks (a), represents (a), embodies, exemplifies, showcases, highlights (as -ing tail), reflects (broader), constitutes, encapsulates.

# Exemplars — worked rewrites and voice calibration

Three levels on each line: the **original draft**, the **de-slopped** version (clean but flat), and the **MBB-snappy** version (clean *and* lands). The third column is the target.

> All targets, sectors and figures below are **illustrative and invented** to teach the move. They are not real findings. In a live deliverable every figure traces to a source (Integrity rule, SKILL.md).

## Worked rewrites

## Voice calibration

The target voice, described by its properties rather than by any one deck: **declarative, compressed, contrast-driven, quantified, and unafraid of a fragment.** Lines that hit the register (illustrative):

- A category claim in three words, full stop.
- "Does your best work. Faster."
- "The real asset is judgement, not the artifact."
- "Built for the workflow, not retro-fitted from a chat tool."
- "Repeatable, but rigid." / "What you delivered, not how."
- "Opinionated where it needs to be; flexible where you are."
- "Cut the per-stage assessment from days to under thirty minutes."

*Tone in one line: state the claim, cut the padding, earn the contrast, and let one short punch carry the slide.*
