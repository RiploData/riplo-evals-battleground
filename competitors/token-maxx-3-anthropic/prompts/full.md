# Writing Guide — De-Slop + Diligence Punch

This guide has four jobs, in order:

0. **Structure** — get the skeleton right before touching the words (the Pyramid Principle).
1. **Cut** — strip the tells out of any draft (the hygiene layer).
2. **Lift** — make what's left snappy, confident and partner-grade (the craft layer).
3. **Reconcile** the layers where they appear to conflict (the judgement layer).

The order matters. Structure first, because no amount of de-slopping rescues a buried argument. Then cut the noise, then lift the signal so it lands. A clean sentence is only half done; the target is clean **and** lands. The canonical voice is the shipped diligence deliverable — the IC/DD body-slide register, dense and clipped. Prose deliverables follow the same concepts at lower compression.

> **Integrity rule (overrides everything below).** Never invent a statistic, fabricate a quote, or manufacture an anecdote to make a line land. Every number traces to the CIM, the data room, or a named source; every customer quote is real and attributable. A flat true sentence beats a snappy false one. When you don't have the figure, say so ("not disclosed — CDD item").

---

## Part 0 — Structure before style (the Pyramid Principle)

**The one rule: lead with the answer.** You *think* bottom-up (data → analysis → conclusion). You *present* top-down (conclusion → arguments → data). State the recommendation first, then support it. Directness reads as confidence.

The pyramid has three levels: the **answer** on top (recommendation / so-what); a handful of **supporting arguments** beneath it (three is classic); **data and evidence** under each.

- **Titles tell the story.** Write titles so reading them alone, top to bottom, gives the whole argument. Titles are *claims*, not *labels*.
- **Group MECE.** Supporting arguments are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive — no overlaps, no gaps. One body slide per argument; give decision-relevant factors a slide, demote the rest to one line. A shipped diligence deck is an exec summary plus two deep-dives on the load-bearing questions — not forty slides giving every factor equal weight.
- **Always produce the one-pager.** Forcing a 6–8 week project onto one page proves you know the key points.
- **When NOT to use it:** an interim fact-share (walking a counterpart through information, not recommending), or a standalone leave-behind meant to stand on its own as longer prose.

## Part 1 — Cut: the tell catalogue

**Rule of thumb: any one of these once may be fine; the tell is several together, or one used repeatedly.**

### Vocabulary
- **The "delve" family** — kill on sight: delve, leverage (verb), utilise, robust, streamline, harness, certainly.
- **Grandiose nouns** for simple ideas: tapestry, landscape, paradigm, synergy, ecosystem.
- **Magic adverbs** that fake significance: quietly, deeply, fundamentally, remarkably, arguably.
- **The "serves as" dodge** — pompous swaps for is/are. "The building *is* a reminder," not "serves as a reminder."
- **Banned openers:** Moreover, Additionally, Furthermore, Notably, Indeed, Importantly. Start with the subject.

### Hedging and filler
- **Soft hedging:** "it's worth noting," "this is where X really shines," length without meaning.
- **The hedging seesaw** — asserting a claim then undercutting it. Pick a position; hedge once, deliberately, with a stated confidence level.
- **Overexplaining the obvious** — spelling out what the reader already knows.

### Manufactured drama
- **Dramatic pivots:** "but here's the thing / the catch / the kicker."
- **False-suspense transitions:** "here's where it gets interesting," "here's what most people miss."
- **"The truth is simple"** — asserting clarity instead of proving it.
- **Self-posed rhetorical question, answered for drama:** "The result? Devastating."
- **"Imagine a world where…"** and grandiose stakes inflation ("will define the next era of computing").

### Openings and endings
- **Throat-clearing:** "let's explore / unpack / dive in," "in this article we'll…" Just start.
- **Gift-wrapped endings:** the restating summary, "in conclusion," "moving forward."
- **"Despite its challenges…":** the fixed optimism beat.

### Rhetoric and structure
- **Fake antithesis:** the two-sentence strawman reveal, "This isn't X. It's Y." (An earned one-line version exists — see Part 3.)
- **"Not X. Not Y. Just Z."** — the dramatic countdown faking a narrowing toward truth.
- **Anaphora abuse** — same sentence opening repeated in quick succession.
- **Tricolon abuse** — rule-of-three stacked back to back. (One per slide is fine.)
- **Uniform staccato** — machine-gun short sentences, every one the same weight. (Deliberate two-beat punch is a craft tool — see Part 3.)
- **Cookie-cutter paragraphs** — every paragraph the same height.
- **Superficial "-ing" analyses** — "highlighting its importance, reflecting broader trends."
- **Invented concept labels** — "the supervision paradox," "the acceleration trap." Names a thing to skip the argument.
- **One-point dilution** — one thesis restated ten ways to feel comprehensive.

### Authenticity
- **Generic examples** — surface praise that fits any company, no insider knowledge.
- **Vague attributions** — unnamed authorities ("experts, observers, industry reports"). If you can't name the source, you don't have one.
- **Dead metaphors** — a metaphor repeated until it dies. Use it once.
- **Passive voice dodging the actor** — "mistakes were made." Name the actor; use the active voice.
- **Invented statistics, quotes and anecdotes** — the Integrity rule; non-negotiable in diligence.

### Formatting
- **Em-dash addiction** — cap at ≤1 per paragraph.
- **Bold-first bullets** — tolerable on slides as labels, but vary it, and never bold a whole sentence.
- **Unicode decoration** — straight characters in body text; arrows OK in slide diagrams only.
- **Suspiciously perfect punctuation** — good writing bends rules when bending them sounds better.

## Part 2 — Lift: the craft

- **Titles carry the takeaway, not the topic.** A claim you could disagree with. Formula: `[Subject] + [active verb] + [specific object]`, optionally + an earned contrast. One idea, ≤14 words. On a body slide use `Kicker: assertion`; carry the caveat in the title where one exists.
- **Two-beat rhythm** — long claim, then short punch (1–4 words). Once per section, on the line that matters most.
- **Earned antithesis — "X, not Y".** Works when Y is a real, specific default the audience holds, compressed to one clause.
- **Compress.** Cut every spare word. Delete each word; if the meaning survives, leave it out. Fragments allowed.
- **Parallelism** for load-bearing triads — same grammatical shape; let structure do the work.
- **Lead with the verb or the claim, never the wind-up.** No "We believe that…".
- **Quantify, and make it concrete.** A concrete comparable beats any adjective. "reduced AI risk assessment to <30min per IC stage" beats "much faster."
- **State confidence — don't pad it.** Declarative, present tense. Hedge the genuinely uncertain claim, calibrated; not every sentence out of reflex.
- **Finding / Rationale / View.** The exec summary runs the assertion, the evidence, and an explicit point of view. The view is the differentiator — judgement, not synthesis.
- **Name the load-bearing dependency out loud** — the condition under which your own conclusion flips. Pre-empts the sharpest pushback.
- **Flag open items; don't bury them.** Name the workstream that closes each one. Calibrated honesty reads as confidence.
- **Score at a glance, and key the score.** Traffic-light verdicts with a legend; no undefined half-states.

## Part 3 — Reconcile: the judgement layer

**The antithesis test.** The catalogue bans fake antithesis; the craft layer is built on earned antithesis. The test: **can the reader name who actually holds position Y?**

| | **Earned contrast — USE it** | **Fake antithesis — CUT it** |
|---|---|---|
| Y is… | a real default / named competitor approach | a strawman nobody believed |
| Length | one compressed line | two sentences: setup, then "correction" |
| Purpose | sharpen positioning | manufacture a reveal |

**Decision rule:** keep the contrast if Y is specific and real and compresses to a single clause. Kill it if it spans two sentences, opens with "This isn't…", or sets up a belief no one held. If you can't name Y in two or three words, you have padding.

**Rhythm.** Uniform short sentences (staccato tell) vs the deliberate two-beat punch (craft): the difference is variation. One punch in a varied paragraph is craft. Five in a row is slop.

**By medium and dosage.** A few prose flags are legitimate slide-craft in moderation:

| Flag | In running prose | On a slide / title |
|---|---|---|
| Short fragments | Avoid as standalone paragraphs | Encouraged ("Repeatable, but rigid."); cap at the one or two that carry weight |
| "X not Y" | Use rarely; apply the test | A core title move; one per title; Y must be nameable |
| Tricolon | One per section, never stacked | One parallel triad per slide is fine |
| Bold-first bullets | Avoid | Fine where the bold word is a genuine label; vary it |
| Unicode arrows | Spell out "to" | Acceptable in slide diagrams (Input → Output) |

**Dosage principle:** any single trope once is fine; the slop is in repetition and stacking. One earned "X, not Y", one two-beat punch and one tricolon per slide reads sharp. Ten of each reads like a machine. Count them.

## Part 4 — Register switch

| Deliverable | Register | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Memo, write-up | Clean prose | Full sentences, varied rhythm |
| IC / DD body slide | Telegraphic | Abbreviations, slashes, fragments; cut every spare word |

Body slides cut far more than running text: "w/o major red flags," "prod/dev ratio," "c. 48%," "+64 NPS," ">114% NRR." On a body slide, if a word can go, it goes. Define each acronym once; don't collapse into jargon-soup. Writing slide content in the prose register is the common mistake.

## Part 5 — Worked rewrites

The **original draft**, the **de-slopped** version (clean but flat), and the **target** (clean *and* lands). The target is usually *shorter* than the de-slopped version.

| Original draft | De-slopped (clean, flat) | Target (lands) |
|---|---|---|
| "My base-case view is that AI is a net positive for this business." | "Our base case is that AI helps this business." | **"AI is the upside here, not the risk. We'd underwrite a buy."** |
| "The proprietary asset is the ratified content library, not the database — 35 years of endorsement…" | "The proprietary asset is the ratified library, not the database. Thirty-five years of endorsement is what an LLM cannot reproduce." | **"The moat is the ratified library, not the code. An LLM can write the schedule; it can't grant itself 35 years of endorsement."** |
| "Schedule authorship requires synthesis of primary legislation… across 12+ statutes that change quarterly." | "Authoring a schedule means synthesising 12-plus statutes that change every quarter." | **"Twelve statutes, changing quarterly, reconciled by hand. Exactly where LLMs break and incumbents win."** |
| "22 FTE technology team → 29 FTE FY27B. Org chart shows zero roles labelled ML/Data Engineer." | "The tech team grows to 29 by FY27B, yet the org chart names no ML engineer." | **"The product works, so the ML capability exists. The org chart just doesn't show it. Find it in the management session."** |

The target column leads with the claim, cuts the connective tissue, lands a short punch, and earns its contrast — without adding words.

## Part 6 — Pre-flight checklist

**Cut:** em-dash ≤1/paragraph · no "but here's the…", "let's dive/unpack", "it's worth noting" · no two-sentence strawman antithesis · no gift-wrap summary · no banned vocabulary or openers · examples specific not generic.

**Lift:** every title a claim you could disagree with, ≤14 words · one two-beat punch on the line that matters · contrasts earned (Y nameable in 2–3 words) · spare words deleted · parallel structure on key triads · leads with verb/claim · concrete and quantified where it counts · hedge only the genuinely uncertain claim.

**If IC / DD body slide:** title is `Kicker: assertion` carrying the caveat · exec summary runs Finding / Rationale / View, and View states a view · telegraphic, acronyms defined once · claims backed by a concrete comparable · load-bearing dependency named · open items flagged with their workstream · scoring has a legend.

**Final gut check:** read the titles alone, top to bottom. Do they tell the whole story? If not, the argument is hiding in the body — pull it up.
