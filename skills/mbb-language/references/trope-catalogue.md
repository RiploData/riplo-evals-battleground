# Extended trope catalogue

A fuller reference layer, drawn from [tropes.fyi](https://tropes.fyi) and the [anti-ai-slop-writing](https://github.com/jalaalrd/anti-ai-slop-writing) skill (which itself draws on Carnegie Mellon 2025, Wikipedia's "Signs of AI writing," and large-scale post analyses). Read alongside `reconciliation.md` C2, which governs the handful that conflict with slide craft.

**Rule of thumb:** any one of these once may be fine; the tell is multiple tropes together, or one used repeatedly.

## Word choice

**Magic adverbs.** Adverbs that fake significance: *quietly, deeply, fundamentally, remarkably, arguably.*
- 🚩 "quietly orchestrating workflows" · "a quiet intelligence behind it"

**"Delve" and friends.** *delve, certainly, utilize, leverage (verb), robust, streamline, harness.*
- 🚩 "Let's delve into the details" · "We need to leverage these robust frameworks."

**Grandiose nouns for simple ideas.** *tapestry, landscape, paradigm, synergy, ecosystem, framework.*
- 🚩 "the rich tapestry of experience" · "navigating the complex landscape of modern AI."

**The "serves as" dodge.** Pompous swaps for "is/are": *serves as, stands as, marks, represents.*
- 🚩 "The platform serves as a reminder of…" → "The platform is…"

## Sentence structure

**Negative parallelism.** "It's not X — it's Y." The single most-identified AI tell. Includes "not because X, but because Y," the em-dash dismissal "X — not Y," and the cross-sentence reframe "The question isn't X. The question is Y." *(An earned, compressed version is a valid slide move — see reconciliation.md.)*
- 🚩 "It's not bold. It's backwards."

**"Not X. Not Y. Just Z."** The dramatic countdown faking a narrowing toward truth.
- 🚩 "Not a bug. Not a feature. A fundamental design flaw."

**"The X? A Y."** Self-posed rhetorical question, answered immediately for drama.
- 🚩 "The result? Devastating." · "The worst part? Nobody saw it coming."

**Anaphora abuse.** Same sentence opening repeated in quick succession.
- 🚩 "They assume users will pay… They assume developers will build… They assume…"

**Tricolon abuse.** Rule-of-three overused or stacked. One is elegant; three back-to-back is a pattern failure. *(One per slide is fine — see reconciliation.md.)*

**"It's worth noting."** Filler transitions that connect nothing: *it bears mentioning, importantly, interestingly, notably.*

**Superficial -ing analyses.** A trailing "-ing" phrase bolting on hollow significance: *highlighting its importance, reflecting broader trends, underscoring its role.*

**False ranges.** "From X to Y" where X and Y aren't on any real scale.
- 🚩 "From innovation to implementation to cultural transformation."

## Paragraph structure

**Short fragments for manufactured emphasis** (in prose). *(Native to slides — see reconciliation.md.)*
- 🚩 "He published this. Openly. In a book."

**Listicle in a trench coat.** A list disguised as prose with "The first… The second… The third…"

## Tone

**False-suspense transitions.** *here's the thing, here's where it gets interesting, here's what most people miss, here's the kicker.*

**"Think of it as…"** The patronizing analogy. Teacher mode; often less clear than the original.

**"Imagine a world where…"** The futurism invitation followed by a wish-list.

**False vulnerability.** Polished, risk-free "honesty" that performs authenticity.
- 🚩 "This is not a rant; it's a diagnosis."

**"The truth is simple."** Asserting clarity instead of proving it.
- 🚩 "The reality is simpler and less flattering."

**Grandiose stakes inflation.** Everything is world-historical.
- 🚩 "This will fundamentally reshape how we think about everything."

**Pedagogical hand-holding.** *let's break this down, let's unpack, let's explore, step by step.*

**Vague attributions.** Unnamed authorities and inflated counts: *experts, observers, industry reports, several publications.* If you can't name the source, you don't have one.

**Invented concept labels.** Abstract problem-nouns appended to domain words and used as if established: *the supervision paradox, the acceleration trap.* Names a thing to skip the argument.

## Formatting

**Em-dash addiction.** 20+ where a human uses 2–3.

**Bold-first bullets.** Every list item opening with a bolded phrase. *(Tolerable on slides as labels; avoid in prose; never bold a whole sentence.)*

**Unicode decoration.** Curly quotes and arrows where straight characters belong. *(Arrows OK in slide diagrams only.)*

## Composition

**Fractal summaries.** "Tell them what you'll tell them / tell them / tell them what you told them" at every level.

**The dead metaphor.** One metaphor beaten flat across the whole piece.

**Historical analogy stacking.** Rapid-fire historical companies/revolutions for borrowed authority.
- 🚩 "Apple didn't build the ride app. The social network didn't build the music app."

**One-point dilution.** One thesis restated ten ways across thousands of words to feel "comprehensive."

**Content duplication.** Whole paragraphs repeated verbatim — an unedited-output tell.

**The signposted conclusion.** "In conclusion / to sum up / in summary."

**"Despite its challenges…"** The fixed beat: "Despite its [positives], [subject] faces challenges… Despite these challenges, [optimism]."

## Additional flags

**Banned sentence openers.** *Certainly, Moreover, Additionally, Furthermore, Notably, Indeed, Importantly.* Start the sentence with its subject instead.

**Hedging seesaw.** Asserting a claim, then undercutting it in the same breath: "This is a major risk, though it may not materialise and could equally be an opportunity." Pick a position. Hedge once, deliberately, with a stated confidence level (High / Medium / Low) — which is exactly what the diligence register already does.

**Corporate pep talk and passive voice.** "Mistakes were made." Name the actor; use the active voice.

**Invented statistics, quotes, anecdotes.** The Integrity rule, repeated as an accuracy failure: never fabricate to strengthen a point. Non-negotiable in diligence.

*Write like a human: varied, imperfect, specific.*
