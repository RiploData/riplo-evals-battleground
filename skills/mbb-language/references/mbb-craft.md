# MBB craft — the sharpening layer

De-slopping removes the tells; it does not make writing *good*. These are the moves that make a clean draft land. The **diligence / IC register comes first** because it's the spine — it's the language that ships to a fund. General craft follows; it applies to all registers.

All examples are illustrative and generic. Where a number appears, treat it as a placeholder — in a real deliverable it must trace to a source (see the Integrity rule in SKILL.md).

---

## Part 1 — The diligence / IC register (the spine)

This is denser and more clipped than prose. When the deliverable is an IC or DD slide, this is the register.

### D1. Title formula: `Kicker: assertion`
Each body slide opens with a short highlighted kicker, a colon, then a full-sentence claim that *is* the takeaway. The strongest titles state the verdict and flag what's still open, in one line — calibrated confidence done in the headline.
- "Executive summary: Limited AI disruption risk; upside across opex, TAM expansion and credentialisation."
- "Moat vs AI: Generative tools can draft the output today, but the regulatory sign-off is what buyers actually pay for."
- "Tech capability: No red flags in delivery to date; team depth and architecture to confirm in TDD."

The third carries both the answer and the caveat — that's the model to copy.

### D2. Lead with the answer; deep-dive only on what matters
An exec summary plus two deep-dives beats forty slides. Pick the load-bearing questions (is the moat real against AI? can the team execute?) and spend the pages there. Everything else is one line in the summary. Pyramid discipline in practice: give the *decision-relevant* factors a slide, demote the rest.

### D3. Three columns: Finding / Rationale / View
The exec summary runs the assertion (finding), the evidence (rationale), and an explicit point of view (the View). The View column is the differentiator — it's judgement, not synthesis. Always carry a View; a diligence reader is paying for the so-what.

### D4. Telegraphic register: compress harder than prose
Body slides cut far more than running text. Abbreviations, slashes and clipped noun-phrases are the default: "w/o major red flags," "prod/dev ratio," "c. 48% margin," "+64 NPS," ">114% NRR," "29 integrations across the main platform categories." On a body slide, if a word can go, it goes. One caveat: define each acronym once, and don't let density collapse into jargon-soup an IC reader can't parse.

### D5. Find a concrete comparable; don't assert
Don't argue "AI won't erode pricing." Lay out a specific analogue set — several adjacent regulated or assurance-led industries — mark where pricing held versus collapsed, and draw the line back to the target. A specific comparable set beats any adjective.

### D6. Name the load-bearing dependency out loud
Close the argument on the condition under which your own conclusion flips: "this only holds if the purchase rationale is assurance; if the buyer's real concern is output quality, pricing comes under pressure." Stating it signals rigour and pre-empts the sharpest pushback before the room raises it.

### D7. Flag open items; don't bury them
Tag rows "further diligence required (org chart, squad set-up, cadence)." Say what you know, mark what you don't, name the workstream that closes it. Calibrated honesty reads as confidence.

### D8. At-a-glance verdict scoring
Traffic-light dots give the skim-reader the verdict per row before they read a word. Always include a legend, and don't use ambiguous half-states (a half-green/half-amber dot) without defining them. Score, but key the score.

## Part 2 — General craft (all registers)

### B1. The action title carries the takeaway, not the topic
Every headline is a **claim you could agree or disagree with**, not a label. The reader should read titles alone and get the argument.
- "AI is reshaping how this category competes" — a position, not "Market context."
- "The defensible asset is the data, not the software" — an argument, not "Our view of the moat."

> **Formula:** `[Subject] + [active verb] + [specific object]`, optionally + an earned contrast. One idea. Aim ≤ 12–14 words. If the title could sit above any slide in any deck, it's a label — rewrite it into a claim.

### B2. Two-beat rhythm — long claim, then short punch
A full clause sets it up; a fragment lands it. The punch is usually 1–4 words.
- "It does your best work. Faster."
- "Every firm has them. Good for credibility, low on value."

This is *not* flag #5 (staccato-on-repeat); it's deliberate, used once, on the line that matters most. Once per slide or section.

### B3. Earned antithesis ("X, not Y") — the core positioning move
- "experience and judgement, not old decks"
- "built for the workflow, not retro-fitted from a chat tool"
- "what you delivered, not how"

It works because Y is a **real, specific default** the audience actually holds, compressed to one line. See reconciliation.md for the rule that separates this from the fake version.

### B4. Compression — fragments and deletions are allowed
Trust fragments; cut every spare word.
- "Repeatable, but rigid."
- "Static, out of date, low value."

Test: delete each word; if the meaning survives, leave it deleted. This is the opposite of hedging (#4) and overexplaining (#11) — same goal, pushed further.

### B5. Parallelism for the load-bearing lists
When three things sit together, give them the same grammatical shape and let the structure do the work.
- "Create / Improve / Standardise" — verb-led, parallel labels.
- "Agents run whole workflows / clients expect more, faster / incumbents aren't catching up" — three subject-verb claims, same cadence.

### B6. Lead with the verb or the claim, never the wind-up
No "We believe that…", no "It is the case that…". Open on the subject doing something. Front-load the load-bearing word.

### B7. Quantify, and make it concrete
Punch comes from specificity. Not "much faster" — "cut the per-stage assessment from days to under 30 minutes." Numbers, named entities and concrete nouns beat adjectives. (This is also the cure for generic examples, flag #12.)

### B8. Confidence — state it, don't pad it
Declarative, present tense, no "may" or "could" unless the uncertainty is the point. In diligence you *will* need calibrated hedges (confidence levels, "subject to"), but hedge **the claim that's genuinely uncertain**, not every sentence out of reflex.
